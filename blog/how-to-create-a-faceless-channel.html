How to Create a Faceless Channel with Google Flow, Suno and DistroKid

Gab · February 2026 · 7 min read

You don't need a camera. You don't need a microphone. You don't need to show your face or record a single thing yourself. With three tools and a few hours, you can launch a faceless channel that publishes music with visuals and distributes to every streaming platform on earth.

Here's exactly how to do it.

What You Need

The whole stack is three tools:

  • Suno — AI music generation. You describe what you want, it creates full songs with vocals, instruments, and production.
  • Google Flow — AI video generation by Google DeepMind. You feed it a prompt and it creates cinematic visuals. Perfect for music videos without a camera crew.
  • DistroKid — Music distribution. Upload your tracks and they go live on Spotify, Apple Music, YouTube Music, Amazon, Tidal, and 150+ other platforms.

Total cost to get started: Suno has a free tier, Google Flow is free during its current rollout, and DistroKid runs about $22/year for unlimited uploads.

Step 1: Create Your Artist Identity

Before you make anything, decide on a brand. This is your faceless identity — the name, the vibe, the genre.

Pick these things first:

  • Artist name (something memorable, easy to search)
  • Genre or niche (lo-fi, synthwave, ambient, chill hip-hop, cinematic — pick one to start)
  • Visual style (dark and moody? bright and dreamy? retro? futuristic?)

Consistency matters more than perfection. People follow channels that have a recognizable feel. Pick a lane and stay in it, at least for your first 10-20 releases.

Step 2: Generate Music with Suno

Go to Suno and start creating tracks. You have two approaches:

Simple mode: Describe what you want in plain English. Something like "a chill lo-fi beat with soft piano and vinyl crackle, 90 bpm, 3 minutes" and Suno generates the full track.

Custom mode: Write your own lyrics, specify the style, set the mood. This gives you more control and makes each track feel more intentional.

Tips for better results:

  • Be specific about instruments, tempo, and mood
  • Generate multiple versions of the same idea and pick the best one
  • Extend tracks that are too short — Suno lets you continue generating from where a song left off
  • Download the stems if you want to do any editing yourself
  • Keep a consistent style across tracks — this builds your channel's identity

Aim to batch-create. Sit down and generate 5-10 tracks in one session. You'll throw away some, keep others. The ones that hit — those are your releases.

Step 3: Create Visuals with Google Flow

This is where it gets cinematic. Google Flow generates video from text prompts. No footage needed, no editing software, no stock video subscriptions.

What works well for music visuals:

  • Slow atmospheric shots — cityscapes at night, rain on windows, forests in fog
  • Abstract motion — flowing colors, particles, geometric patterns
  • Character-driven scenes — a silhouette walking through neon streets, someone sitting by a window
  • Nature loops — ocean waves, clouds moving, aurora borealis

Prompt example:

Cinematic slow-motion shot of rain falling on a neon-lit Tokyo street at night. No people. Reflections on wet pavement. Moody, atmospheric. 4K quality.

Generate multiple clips and combine them. You want the visuals to match the energy of your track — calm music gets slow, dreamy visuals. Upbeat tracks get more dynamic movement.

For YouTube, you'll want your video to match your audio length. Either loop your best clips or generate enough to cover the full track. A simple video editor like CapCut or even iMovie is enough to sync everything up.

Step 4: Combine Audio + Video

Open any video editor — CapCut, DaVinci Resolve (free), or iMovie. Drop in your Google Flow visuals and your Suno audio track. Line them up.

Keep it simple:

  • Add your artist name and track title as minimal text overlay
  • Use cross-dissolve transitions between clips
  • Match visual transitions to the music's natural breaks
  • Export at 1080p or 4K for YouTube

That's your music video. No camera, no crew, no studio. Just AI-generated audio paired with AI-generated visuals.

Step 5: Distribute with DistroKid

DistroKid is how your music gets on every streaming platform. Sign up, pick the musician plan, and start uploading.

For each release, you'll need:

  • The audio file (WAV or high-quality MP3)
  • Cover art (1400x1400 or 3000x3000 — you can generate this with any AI image tool)
  • Track title, artist name, genre
  • Release date (schedule releases ahead for consistency)

DistroKid pushes your track to Spotify, Apple Music, YouTube Music, Amazon Music, Tidal, Deezer, and 150+ other platforms. It usually goes live within 1-3 days.

For YouTube specifically: Upload the full music video directly to your YouTube channel. Use the audio-only version through DistroKid for YouTube Music.

Step 6: Build the Channel

Now you repeat the process. Consistency is the whole game.

A realistic release schedule:

  • 1-2 tracks per week on streaming platforms
  • 1 music video per week on YouTube
  • Batch-create on weekends, schedule releases throughout the week

Growth tactics that work for faceless channels:

  • Use YouTube Shorts — clip 30-60 second highlights from your videos
  • Create playlists on Spotify around your genre/mood
  • Cross-post clips to TikTok and Instagram Reels
  • Engage with other creators in your niche
  • Submit to Spotify editorial playlists through DistroKid

Revenue Streams

A faceless channel can earn from multiple angles:

  • Spotify/Apple Music streams — small per-stream payouts add up with volume
  • YouTube ad revenue — once you hit 1,000 subscribers and 4,000 watch hours
  • YouTube Music — additional streaming revenue from your distributed tracks
  • Sync licensing — content creators may want to use your music in their videos

Nobody gets rich from their first 10 tracks. But 50 tracks across all platforms with consistent visuals and a clear niche? That starts compounding.

The Whole Workflow in One Glance

  1. Pick your artist name, genre, and visual style
  2. Generate tracks with Suno (batch 5-10 at a time)
  3. Generate matching visuals with Google Flow
  4. Combine audio + video in a simple editor
  5. Upload to YouTube with proper metadata
  6. Distribute audio to all platforms via DistroKid
  7. Repeat weekly

Three tools, no face, no camera, no mic. You're a music publisher now.
