Beta

Risk Metric Investment Wiki — Fundamentals
Beta is a measure of a stock's volatility in relation to the overall market (typically the S&P 500). It helps investors understand the systematic risk profile of a security; a beta greater than 1.0 indicates higher volatility than the market, while a beta less than 1.0 implies lower volatility.

TypeRisk Metric

1.0 The Formula

Basic Form

formulaβ = Covariance(Rs, Rm) / Variance(Rm)

Where:
Rs = Return of the individual stock
Rm = Return of the market index

The formula compares how a stock's returns move relative to the market's returns. Covariance measures how the two variables (stock price and market index) move together, while Variance measures how far the market's data points are spread out from their average. These figures are calculated using historical price data, not from the Balance Sheet or Income Statement.

Worked Example

CompanyVolatility ProfileCorrelation to MarketBeta
Company A (Utility)Low Price SwingsHigh (0.8)0.5
Company B (Tech)High Price SwingsHigh (0.8)1.5

Company A has a Beta of 0.5, meaning it theoretically moves only 50% as much as the market (a defensive stock). Company B has a Beta of 1.5, suggesting it is 50% more volatile than the market. Aggressive investors might prefer B during bull markets to amplify gains, while conservative investors would choose A to preserve capital.

High beta doesn't always mean "better returns". It means "more movement". In a market crash, high beta stocks typically fall much harder than low beta stocks.

2.0 Interpretation & Edge Cases

Benchmarks

• β = 1: The stock moves in lockstep with the market.
• β > 1: The stock is more volatile (riskier) than the market. Examples include tech startups and cyclicals.
• β < 1: The stock is less volatile (safer) than the market. Examples include utilities and consumer staples.
• β < 0: The stock moves inversely to the market (rare, e.g., gold or inverse ETFs).

When it Fails (Pitfalls)

Beta is strictly backward-looking based on historical data. Past volatility does not guarantee future risk. A company can have a low historical beta but face a new, catastrophic risk (e.g., bankruptcy) that the historical data hasn't captured yet. It also assumes market efficiency, which is not always the case.

Do not use Beta in isolation for valuation. It is a risk metric, not a quality metric. A terrible company can have a low beta simply because it doesn't trade often (liquidity risk) or has uncorrelated movements.
