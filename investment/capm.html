CAPM (Capital Asset Pricing Model)

Valuation Model Investment Wiki — Fundamentals
The Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) is a formula used to calculate the expected return of an asset based on its systematic risk (beta) and the expected market return. It helps investors determine if an investment is fairly valued given its risk profile.
Quick Reference
TypeValuation Model
FormulaER = Rf + β(Rm – Rf)

1.0 The Formula

Basic Form

formulaExpected Return = Risk-Free Rate + [Beta × (Market Return – Risk-Free Rate)]

Where:
Rf = Risk-Free Rate (usually 10y Treasury Yield)
β = Beta of the investment
Rm = Expected Return of the Market (e.g., S&P 500 average)

CAPM breaks down return into two parts: the risk-free return (what you'd get from a safe government bond) and the risk premium (the extra return you demand for taking on the volatility of the stock market). The term (Rm – Rf) is known as the Equity Risk Premium.

Worked Example

CompanyBetaRisk-Free Rate / Market ReturnRequired Return (CAPM)
Company A (Stable)0.84% / 10%8.8%
Company B (Risky)1.54% / 10%13.0%

Company A is less volatile (Beta 0.8), so investors require an 8.8% return to hold it. Company B is riskier (Beta 1.5), so investors demand a higher return of 13.0% to compensate for that risk. If you believe Company B will only return 10% in reality, it is theoretically overvalued according to CAPM because the return doesn't justify the risk.

The higher the Beta, the higher the discount rate you must use in your valuation models. This lowers the present value of future cash flows, making high-growth, high-risk companies harder to justify unless their projected growth is explosive.

2.0 Interpretation & Edge Cases

Benchmarks

• High Result: Implies the stock is risky and must generate significant returns to be attractive.
• Low Result: Implies the stock is safer and requires less return to be viable.
• Comparison: Compare the CAPM result to your own estimated ROI. If Estimated ROI > CAPM, the stock might be undervalued (offering alpha).

When it Fails (Pitfalls)

CAPM relies heavily on Beta, which is historical and may not predict future risk. It also requires estimating the Expected Market Return (Rm), which is subjective. A small change in the Risk-Free Rate or Market Premium assumption can drastically change the output.

Do not use CAPM as a prediction of what the stock WILL do. It is a calculation of what the stock SHOULD do to justify its risk. Real-world returns often deviate significantly from CAPM predictions.
