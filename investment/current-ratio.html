Current Ratio

Liquidity Ratio Investment Wiki — Fundamentals
The Current Ratio is a liquidity metric that measures a company's ability to pay short-term obligations or those due within one year. It tells investors if a company has enough assets to cover its debts.
Quick Reference
TypeLiquidity Ratio
FormulaCurrent Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities
Good Target1.5 to 2.0 (Ideally > 1.0)
Best Used ForMeasuring short-term financial health and bankruptcy risk

1.0 The Formula

Basic Form

formulaCurrent Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities

Where:
Current Assets = Cash, Inventory, Receivables
Current Liabilities = Debt due within 1 year, Accounts Payable

Both components are found on the Balance Sheet. Current Assets represent resources that can be converted to cash within a year. Current Liabilities represent debts that must be paid within a year.

Worked Example

CompanyCurrent AssetsCurrent LiabilitiesCurrent Ratio
Company A$2,000,000$1,000,0002.0
Company B$800,000$1,000,0000.8

Company A has $2.00 in assets for every $1.00 of debt, meaning it is financially stable in the short term. Company B has only $0.80 for every dollar of debt, suggesting it may struggle to pay bills and could face a liquidity crisis.

A ratio under 1.0 is a red flag, but a ratio that is too high (e.g., > 3.0) can also be bad, indicating that management is hoarding cash instead of reinvesting it efficiently.

2.0 Interpretation & Edge Cases

Benchmarks

• Ratio < 1.0: Warning sign. The company has more short-term debt than assets.
• Ratio = 1.0 - 1.5: Acceptable, but tight.
• Ratio = 1.5 - 2.5: Healthy range.
• Ratio > 3.0: Potentially inefficient use of capital.

When it Fails (Pitfalls)

The Current Ratio includes Inventory, which might not be easily sold (liquidated) in a crisis. If a company sells specialized equipment, its inventory might be worthless in a fire sale. For a stricter test, use the Quick Ratio.

Do not compare Current Ratios across different industries. Retailers naturally have different capital structures than software companies.
