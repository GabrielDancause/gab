Debt-to-Equity Ratio

Leverage Ratio Investment Wiki — Fundamentals
The Debt-to-Equity (D/E) ratio is a leverage metric used to evaluate a company's financial leverage. It calculates the weight of total debt and liabilities against the shareholders' equity.
Quick Reference
TypeLeverage Ratio
FormulaD/E = Total Liabilities / Shareholders' Equity
Good TargetIndustry dependent; generally < 1.0 is healthy
Best Used ForAssessing long-term solvency and leverage

1.0 The Formula

Basic Form

formulaDebt-to-Equity = Total Liabilities / Shareholders' Equity

Where:
Total Liabilities = Short-term Debt + Long-term Debt + Other Liabilities
Shareholders' Equity = Total Assets - Total Liabilities

Both components are found on the Balance Sheet. Liabilities represent what the company owes to outsiders. Equity represents the net value belonging to owners/shareholders. This ratio tells you how much debt is used to finance every dollar of equity.

Worked Example

CompanyTotal LiabilitiesShareholders' EquityDebt-to-Equity Ratio
Company A$500,000$1,000,0000.5
Company B$2,000,000$1,000,0002.0

Company A has a D/E of 0.5, meaning it uses twice as much equity as debt to fund operations—a conservative structure. Company B has a D/E of 2.0, meaning it is heavily leveraged with $2 of debt for every $1 of equity. In a downturn, Company B faces much higher bankruptcy risk.

Leverage acts as a magnifier. In good times, high leverage amplifies Returns on Equity (ROE). In bad times, it amplifies losses and creates solvency risk.

2.0 Interpretation & Edge Cases

Benchmarks

• D/E < 1.0: Generally considered safe and conservative.
• D/E > 2.0: Indicates high leverage and higher risk (unless in utilities/telecom).
• Negative D/E: Indicates the company has more liabilities than assets (insolvency warning).

When it Fails (Pitfalls)

Capital-intensive industries (like Utilities, Telecom, Manufacturing) naturally have higher D/E ratios (often 2.0+) because they require massive infrastructure investment. Always compare companies within the same sector.

Watch out for "Share Buybacks". A company buying back its own stock reduces Shareholders' Equity, which mathematically increases the D/E ratio even if debt hasn't increased. This can artificially inflate the risk profile.
