Amcor (AMCR)

Company Analysis Investment Wiki — Fundamentals
Amcor plc (AMCR) is a global leader in developing and producing responsible packaging for food, beverage, pharmaceutical, medical, home- and personal-care, and other products. With a strong focus on sustainability and innovation, Amcor operates in over 40 countries. It is known for its defensive business model, consistent dividend growth, and resilience during economic downturns.

1.0 Business Overview

Amcor generates revenue through two primary reporting segments, providing packaging solutions that protect products and extend shelf life.

Flexibles

The largest segment by revenue. Amcor Flexibles provides flexible packaging solutions globally. This includes:

  • Food & Beverage: Wrappers, pouches, and bags for fresh and processed foods.
  • Healthcare: Sterile packaging for medical devices and pharmaceuticals.
  • Personal Care: Packaging for cosmetics and toiletries.

Rigid Packaging

Focused primarily on the Americas. This segment manufactures rigid containers, including:

  • Beverage Containers: PET bottles for water, soda, and sports drinks (working with major brands like PepsiCo and Coca-Cola).
  • Specialty Containers: Jars and bottles for food, spirits, and home care products.

2.0 Financial Performance

Competitive Moat

Amcor possesses a narrow but durable moat driven by Economies of Scale and Switching Costs. Its global manufacturing footprint allows it to serve multinational giants (like PepsiCo and Nestlé) across all their markets, a capability few competitors can match. Furthermore, integration into customer production lines and long-term supply contracts make switching suppliers costly and operationally risky.

Stable Cash Flows

Due to the essential nature of its end markets (food, healthcare, hygiene), Amcor's demand remains relatively stable even during recessions. This predictability supports strong free cash flow generation (FCF).

Dividend Consistency

Amcor is often favored by income investors. The company has a long history of increasing its annual dividend, qualifying it as a "Dividend Aristocrat" (based on its combined history with Bemis and legacy Amcor listings). It targets a payout ratio that allows for sustainable growth while reinvesting in the business.

4.0 Risks

Raw Material Costs

Fluctuations in the price of resin (derived from oil and natural gas) can impact short-term profitability, although pass-through contracts mitigate this over time.

Sustainability & Regulation

The global push against single-use plastics is a significant long-term risk. Amcor is addressing this by investing heavily in:

  • Recyclability: Aiming for all packaging to be recyclable or reusable by 2025.
  • Lightweighting: Reducing the amount of material used in packaging.
  • Recycled Content: Incorporating more PCR (Post-Consumer Recycled) resin.

Foreign Exchange

As a global company reporting in USD, Amcor faces currency headwinds when the dollar strengthens against other currencies where it operates.
